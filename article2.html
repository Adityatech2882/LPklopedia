Xero Demo Tape 1 1997

Demo Cassette Tape 1 (1997) is one of the earliest known recordings by Xero, the band that would later

become Linkin Park. Recorded in 1996 and released in 1997, this demo captures the group during their

formative years, long before achieving mainstream success.

Background

The origins of the demo trace back to the friendship between Mike Shinoda and Mark Wakefield, who had

known each other since childhood. Both shared a passion for music but came from different influences

Wakefield leaned toward rock bands like Rage Against the Machine and Nirvana, while Shinoda was deeply

influenced by hip-hop artists such as Tupac and Wu-Tang Clan.

After forming Xero around 1995-1996, they began experimenting with blending rap and rock elements. Soon

after, Brad Delson joined the project, helping record some of the earliest material.

This demo tape was created as part of their early efforts to develop a unique sound and gain attention from

industry professionals.

The Demo Cassette

Demo Cassette Tape 1 is a self-titled release and is notable for being the earliest known Xero demo with

official artwork.

The existence of this tape remained largely unknown to the public until 2020, when it was revealed through

the book One Step Closer: From Xero to #1: Becoming Linkin Park by Jeff Blue, a former Warner Bros.

Records executive.

The cassette itself featured simple, low-budget packaging, reportedly including a Xerox-style image on the

cover and photos of the band members typical of underground demos from that era.

Transition to Linkin Park

After releasing the demo, Xero struggled to secure a record deal. A major turning point came when Mark

Wakefield left the band. They later recruited Chester Bennington, whose powerful and emotional vocals

transformed their sound.

Musical Style

The sound of Demo Cassette Tape 1 is raw and unpolished, combining elements of hip-hop beats, rap vocals,

and alternative rock instrumentation. Mike Shinoda handled much of the rapping, while Mark Wakefielde

contributed melodic vocals.

Compared to later Linkin Park material, the production is minimal and experimental, lacking the refined

structure heard in albums like Hybrid Theory. However, the demo clearly shows the foundation of their

future sound.

Legacy and Importance

Although Demo Cassette Tape 1 never received an official wide release, it is considered a crucial piece of

Linkin Park history. It documents the band's earliest creative direction and their attempt to merge genres in

a way that would later define their global success.

Today, the tape is extremely rare and highly valued among fans and collectors. More importantly, it serves as

a reminder that even one of the biggest bands in modern rock started with small, DIY recordings and

experimentation.
